If you've ever searched for your own brand on Google and seen a competitor's ad sitting above your organic listing, you've already experienced brand bidding first-hand. It's one of the most contentious practices in paid search — and one of the most effective.

This guide breaks down exactly what brand bidding is, how it works, whether it's legal, and what you can do about it.

## What is brand bidding?

Brand bidding is when an advertiser uses another company's brand name as a keyword in Google Ads. The goal is simple: intercept people who are already searching for a specific brand and redirect them elsewhere.

Here's a concrete example. When someone types "Nike shoes" into Google, Adidas could bid on that keyword and show their ad above Nike's organic listing. The searcher was looking for Nike, but the first thing they see is a paid ad from a competitor.

This isn't limited to huge companies. It happens across every industry — SaaS, financial services, e-commerce, local trades, legal services. If you have a brand name that people search for, someone can bid on it.

## How brand bidding works technically

Understanding the mechanics helps you spot it and respond to it. There are four things to know.

### Keyword targeting

The advertiser adds your brand name (or variations of it) to their Google Ads keyword list. This tells Google: "When someone searches for this term, I want my ad to be eligible to appear."

They might target your exact brand name, your brand plus a product category ("Acme CRM pricing"), or common misspellings.

### Match types matter

How the keyword is configured changes how aggressively it triggers:

- **Exact match** `[acme crm]` — The ad only shows when someone searches for that precise term or very close variants.
- **Phrase match** `"acme crm"` — The ad shows when the search includes that phrase, even with words before or after it.
- **Broad match** `acme crm` — Google may show the ad for searches it considers related, which can cast a wide net.

Most brand bidders use exact or phrase match to keep their targeting tight and their spend efficient.

### Ad copy rules

This is where many people get confused. Google draws a clear line:

- **You CAN bid on a trademarked keyword.** There is no rule against it.
- **You CANNOT use a trademark in your ad copy** without authorisation from the trademark holder (with some exceptions, such as resellers, informational sites, and comparison services in certain regions).

So a competitor can bid on your brand name and show an ad — but they typically cannot write "Better than Acme CRM" or "Official Acme Alternative" in the ad text itself if "Acme" is a registered trademark and you've filed a complaint with Google.

### Quality Score implications

When you bid on a competitor's brand, your ad is inherently less relevant. The searcher wanted Brand X, not you. This means:

- Your Quality Score on that keyword will usually be low
- Your cost per click will be higher than the brand owner's
- Your click-through rate will be lower

Despite this, brand bidding often delivers strong conversion rates — because the people clicking are high-intent buyers who are actively evaluating options.

## Is brand bidding legal?

Yes. In most jurisdictions, bidding on a competitor's brand name as a keyword is perfectly legal.

### Google's trademark policy

Google explicitly permits advertisers to bid on trademarked terms as keywords. Their policy only restricts the use of trademarks within ad text. If a trademark owner files a complaint, Google may prevent other advertisers from using the trademarked term in their headlines and descriptions — but the keyword targeting itself remains untouched.

### UK and EU legal precedent

The most significant case in this area is **Interflora v Marks & Spencer** (Court of Justice of the European Union, 2011). M&S had been bidding on "Interflora" as a keyword in Google Ads.

The court ruled that bidding on a competitor's trademark as a keyword is not, in itself, trademark infringement. It only becomes infringement if the resulting ad is likely to mislead consumers about the identity of the advertiser — in other words, if people might think the ad is from the trademark owner when it isn't.

This ruling has shaped how brand bidding is treated across Europe. The keyword itself is fair game. The ad copy is where liability can arise.

### Where it crosses the line

Brand bidding becomes legally problematic when:

- The ad copy creates confusion about who the advertiser actually is
- The ad uses the competitor's trademark in a way that suggests an official relationship
- The landing page is designed to impersonate the brand

In practice, most brand bidders are careful to avoid these issues. They want the traffic, not a lawsuit.

## Why companies bid on competitor brands

Brand bidding is popular because it works. Here's why advertisers do it.

### Intercepting high-intent traffic

Someone searching for your brand name is often at the bottom of the funnel. They know what they want. They may be about to sign up, buy, or request a quote. A competitor's ad appearing at that exact moment can redirect a sale that was almost yours.

### Lower customer acquisition costs

Brand-term searches typically convert at much higher rates than generic keywords. Even though the CPC is inflated by the lack of relevance, the cost per acquisition can still be lower than bidding on non-branded terms.

### Competitive positioning

Showing up when someone searches for your competitor is a powerful positioning move. It says: "Before you commit, consider this alternative." For challenger brands, this is one of the most effective ways to get in front of exactly the right audience.

### Affiliate brand bidding

This is a particular nuisance. Affiliates who earn commission by driving sales to your website may bid on your brand name to capture traffic that was already heading to you. You end up paying commission on sales you would have got anyway. It's a genuine drain on margins, and it's surprisingly common.

## The impact on the brand being targeted

If competitors or affiliates are bidding on your brand, the effects are real and measurable.

### Increased CPCs on your own brand terms

When competitors bid on your brand, you're no longer the only advertiser in the auction. Competition drives up your cost per click, even on terms you should own cheaply.

### Lost impression share

You may not appear for every search of your own brand name. Google's auction decides who shows, and if a competitor is bidding aggressively, your ads may not always be visible.

### Revenue leakage

Every click a competitor captures on your brand search is a potential customer diverted. Over time, this adds up. For high-traffic brands, even a small percentage of lost clicks translates to significant lost revenue.

### Wasted defensive spend

Many brands respond by running their own brand campaigns — paying for clicks they would have received organically for free. This is rational (you need to defend your SERP position), but it's still money you shouldn't have to spend.

## How to detect brand bidding

You can't fix what you can't see. Here are the main ways to detect whether someone is bidding on your brand.

### Google Ads Auction Insights

If you're already running ads on your own brand terms, the Auction Insights report in Google Ads will show you which other advertisers are appearing alongside your ads. You'll see their impression share, overlap rate, and position relative to yours.

The limitation: this only works if you're already running brand campaigns. And it shows who appears alongside you, not who appears when you don't.

### Manual SERP searches

The most obvious approach — just Google yourself. But this is unreliable for several reasons:

- Ads vary by location, device, time of day, and user history
- Google personalises results, so what you see may not be what your customers see
- Competitors may only bid during certain hours or in certain regions
- You'd need to search consistently across multiple locations to get accurate data

It's a good starting point, but it won't give you the full picture.

## How to respond to brand bidding

Once you've confirmed that someone is bidding on your brand, you have several options.

### File a trademark complaint with Google

If the competitor is using your trademark in their ad copy (not just as a keyword), you can file a trademark complaint through Google's advertising policies. Google will review the ad and, if it violates their policy, restrict the advertiser from using your trademark in their text.

This doesn't stop them bidding on the keyword — only from using your brand name in the ad itself. But it does reduce the effectiveness of their ads significantly.

### Optimise your organic listing

A strong organic presence on your brand terms is your first line of defence. Make sure your:

- Title tag includes your brand name prominently
- Meta description is compelling and up to date
- Site links are appearing in your organic listing
- Google Business Profile (if applicable) is complete

If your organic listing is strong, many searchers will scroll past the paid ads to find you.

### Monitor and defend selectively

Not every instance of brand bidding warrants a response. If a competitor is bidding on your brand but getting minimal impression share, the cost of running a defensive campaign may outweigh the revenue you're losing.

The key is data. Know who is bidding, how often they appear, and what impact it's having. Then make a calculated decision about whether to defend.
