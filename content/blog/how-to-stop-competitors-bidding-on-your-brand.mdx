# How to Stop Competitors Bidding on Your Brand Name in Google Ads

You search your own brand name in Google and there it is — a competitor's ad sitting above your organic listing, siphoning off the traffic you spent years building. It is frustrating, it feels unfair, and your first instinct is to find a way to make it stop.

Here is the uncomfortable truth: you cannot fully prevent competitors from bidding on your brand name in Google Ads. But you are not powerless either. There are concrete steps you can take to protect your brand, reduce the damage, and — if you are smart about it — turn the situation to your advantage.

## What Is Brand Bidding and Why Do Competitors Do It?

Brand bidding is when a competitor targets your brand name as a keyword in their Google Ads campaigns. When someone searches for "Acme Software", a competitor like "Rival Software" can bid on that exact term and show their ad above your organic result.

Competitors do this for three reasons:

1. **Your brand traffic is high-intent.** Someone searching your name already knows who you are. They are close to converting. That makes them incredibly valuable to a competitor offering an alternative.

2. **It inflates your costs.** If you run your own brand campaigns, a competitor entering the auction drives up your cost-per-click. We have seen brand CPCs jump from 20p to over £1.50 when a competitor enters the auction — a 7x increase on clicks you would have got organically for free.

3. **It diverts customers at the point of decision.** Even if only 5-10% of searchers click the competitor's ad instead of your listing, that can represent thousands of pounds in lost revenue each month for established brands.

## Can You Actually Stop It?

Let's be direct: **Google explicitly allows competitors to bid on your brand name as a keyword.** This is not a loophole — it is Google's stated policy. You cannot file a complaint to stop someone targeting your brand as a keyword.

However, there is an important distinction between keywords and ad copy.

### What You Can Do: Trademark Complaints for Ad Copy Violations

While competitors can bid on your brand name as a keyword, they **cannot** use your trademarked brand name in their ad text without authorisation. If a competitor's ad says "Better than Acme Software" or "Acme Alternative — Try Us Free", and "Acme" is your registered trademark, you have grounds for a complaint.

Google's trademark policy (updated February 2025) requires you to file complaints against specific ads. Google no longer lets you proactively block all advertisers from using your trademark — you must identify and report individual violations.

To file a trademark complaint:

- Go to the [Google Ads Trademark Complaint Form](https://services.google.com/inquiry/aw_trademark)
- Provide your trademark registration details
- Identify the specific ads that violate your trademark
- Google typically reviews complaints within two to three weeks

If upheld, Google will remove the trademarked term from the offending ad copy. The competitor can still bid on your brand keyword — they just cannot mention your name in their ads.

### Legal Options: Cease and Desist Letters

If a competitor is persistently bidding on your brand and using your name in their ad copy, a formal cease and desist letter can be effective. Many businesses will back off when they receive legal correspondence, because the potential liability outweighs the benefit of a few extra clicks.

### What About Gentleman's Agreements?

In some industries, competitors agree not to bid on each other's brand terms. These agreements have no legal enforceability, but they can work — particularly in tight-knit verticals where relationships matter. The problem is they fall apart the moment a new player enters the market or someone quietly breaks the agreement.

## Defensive Strategies That Actually Work

### 1. Run Your Own Brand Campaign (But Understand the Trade-Off)

The most common advice you will hear is "run your own brand campaign so you always appear at the top." This works, but it comes with a cost that is rarely discussed honestly.

Research from Branding Brand and multiple industry studies suggests that **roughly 80% of paid brand clicks would have happened organically anyway.** You are essentially paying Google for traffic you already own. For a brand getting 10,000 brand searches per month at a £0.30 CPC, that is £2,400 per month — £28,800 per year — mostly spent on clicks you would have received for free.

Running a permanent brand campaign "just in case" is like keeping every light in your house on because you might walk into a dark room. It works, but it is wasteful.

### 2. Strengthen Your Organic Brand Presence

A strong organic presence reduces the impact of competitor ads. Focus on:

- **Sitelinks:** Ensure Google shows sitelinks under your organic listing. They take up more SERP real estate and push competitor ads further down.
- **Knowledge panel:** Claim and optimise your Google Business Profile so your brand panel appears on the right side of results.
- **Rich snippets:** Use structured data markup (review stars, FAQ schema) to make your organic listing more prominent.
- **Site reputation:** The stronger your domain authority, the more confidently Google displays enhanced organic features for your brand.

When your organic listing dominates the SERP with sitelinks, a knowledge panel, and rich snippets, a single competitor ad above it becomes far less effective.

### 3. Monitor and React Instead of Always Defending

This is where most brands get the strategy wrong. They either ignore the problem entirely or run permanent brand campaigns that burn budget every day.

The smarter approach is **reactive defence**: monitor your brand SERPs continuously and only activate brand campaigns when competitors actually appear.

Think about it. If competitors only bid on your brand intermittently — perhaps during their own promotional pushes or when they are testing new campaigns — there is no reason to spend money on brand ads during the 80% of the time when no competitor is present.

## The Smarter Approach: Monitor First, Spend Second

Here is how a reactive brand protection strategy works in practice:

**When no competitors are present:** Your organic listing sits at the top of your brand SERP. Searchers click through to your site at no cost. Your ad budget goes entirely toward non-brand growth campaigns — the searches where paid ads actually generate incremental traffic.

**When a competitor appears:** You get an alert, review the competitor's ad, and make a decision. Maybe you activate your brand campaign for that period. Maybe you file a trademark complaint if they are using your name in ad copy. Maybe their ad is so weak it is not worth responding to.

This approach typically saves brands 60-80% of what they would spend on a permanent brand campaign, while maintaining the same level of protection.

You cannot stop competitors from bidding on your brand name in Google Ads. That is Google's policy, and no amount of frustration will change it. What you can control is how you respond.
