## The Standard Advice (and Why It's Wrong)

Ask any PPC agency whether you should bid on your own brand name and you'll get the same answer: "Absolutely. Always. Without question."

They'll tell you it's defensive. They'll tell you competitors will steal your traffic. They'll tell you the CPCs are so cheap it's basically free money. And they'll point to the impressive ROAS figures in your brand campaign as proof it's working.

Here's what they won't mention: they have a direct financial incentive to keep you spending. Brand campaigns are the easiest campaigns to manage. They produce the best-looking numbers in client reports. And they pad out monthly spend, which is often how agency fees are calculated.

The real answer to whether you should bid on your own brand name is not "always" or "never." It depends on your specific situation, and the only way to get it right is to look at the data honestly.

## What the Data Actually Says

### Google's Own Research Says 80% of Brand Clicks Are Wasted

Google published incrementality studies examining what happens when advertisers pause their brand campaigns. The findings were striking: roughly 80% of clicks on brand ads are not incremental. That means those users would have clicked your organic listing anyway. You paid for a click you were going to get for free.

Let that sink in. Four out of five people clicking your brand ad were already looking for you. They typed your name into Google. They were coming to your site regardless. You just paid Google for the privilege of being in their way.

### Brand ROAS Is a Vanity Metric

A 10x ROAS on your brand campaign looks phenomenal in a monthly report. But what is it actually measuring? It's measuring how many people who already knew your brand name and were actively searching for you went on to buy something.

That's not demand creation. That's demand capture. And it's demand you were going to capture anyway through your organic listing.

Compare that to a non-brand campaign with a 3x ROAS. Which one is actually driving growth? The non-brand campaign is reaching people who didn't know you existed. The brand campaign is taxing people who already did.

### The Real Cost of Getting This Wrong

Let's put numbers on it. Say you're spending £2,000 per month on brand campaigns. If Google's incrementality data holds and 80% of those clicks are cannibalised from organic, you're burning £1,600 every month.

That's £19,200 per year spent acquiring customers who were already yours.

Now imagine redirecting that £1,600 per month into non-brand campaigns. Even at a modest 3x ROAS, that's an additional £4,800 per month in revenue from genuinely new customers. Over a year, that's £57,600 in revenue you left on the table.

## When You SHOULD Bid on Your Brand Name

None of this means brand bidding is always wrong. There are clear situations where it makes sense.

### Competitors Are Actively Bidding on Your Terms

If you search your brand name and see a competitor's ad sitting above your organic listing, you have a problem. They're intercepting people who were looking for you and redirecting them. In this case, brand bidding is genuinely defensive — you're protecting traffic that would otherwise be lost to a competitor, not cannibalising your own organic clicks.

The key word here is "actively." Don't assume competitors are bidding on your terms. Check. Search your brand name in an incognito window. Look at the auction insights report in Google Ads. If no one else is there, you're fighting a battle that doesn't exist.

### You Have a Specific Promotion to Push

Running a flash sale? Launching a new product? Brand ads let you control the message at the top of the SERP. Your organic listing might still show your standard meta description, but a brand ad can highlight the specific offer. This is a tactical, time-limited use — not a permanent campaign.

### Your Organic Listing Is Weak

If your organic listing for your brand name lacks sitelinks, has a poor meta description, or doesn't dominate the top of the page, brand ads can fill the gap. But the better long-term solution is to fix your organic presence. Proper schema markup, a well-optimised homepage, and building enough authority for Google to grant sitelinks will serve you better than paying for ads indefinitely.

### You're in a Highly Contested Market

Some industries are brutal for brand terms. Insurance, finance, legal services — these are sectors where competitors routinely bid on each other's brand names as standard practice. If you're in one of these markets, brand bidding may be a cost of doing business. But even then, you should measure the incremental value rather than assuming it's worth the spend.

## When You Should NOT Bid on Your Brand Name

### You Rank Number One Organically With Sitelinks

If you search your brand name and your organic listing dominates the page — position one with sitelinks, maybe a knowledge panel on the right — there's very little for a brand ad to add. You're already occupying the most prominent real estate on the page. Running ads on top of this is paying for clicks you'd get anyway.

### No Competitors Are Bidding on Your Terms

This is the simplest test. If you search your brand name and no competitor ads appear, who exactly are you defending against? You're bidding against yourself, driving up costs for no reason.

### Your Branded CPC Is Inflated

When you bid on your own brand terms and no one else does, Google still charges you a CPC. And sometimes that CPC creeps up over time, especially if you've been running broad match brand campaigns or have poor quality scores. Check your actual branded CPCs — you might be surprised.

### The Budget Would Be Better Spent on Acquisition

This is the opportunity cost argument, and it's the most compelling one. Every pound spent on brand campaigns is a pound not spent on reaching new customers. If you're in a position where brand ads aren't adding incremental value, that budget is doing nothing. Move it to non-brand campaigns where it can actually drive growth.

## The Third Option: Monitor and React

Most articles on brand bidding present it as a binary choice — always on or always off. But there's a smarter approach: monitor your brand SERPs and only activate brand campaigns when there's an actual threat.

### Don't Guess. Know.

The biggest risk of pausing brand campaigns is that a competitor starts bidding on your terms and you don't notice. By the time you spot it in your traffic reports, you've already lost clicks and conversions.

The solution isn't to keep paying for brand ads "just in case." The solution is automated monitoring. Track your brand SERPs daily. Get alerted the moment a competitor ad appears on your brand terms. Then — and only then — switch your brand campaigns on.

## How to Test It Yourself

Don't take our word for it — or your agency's. Run the test yourself.

### The Geo-Based Incrementality Test

This is the gold standard for measuring brand campaign incrementality, and any advertiser can do it.

**Step 1:** Split your target locations into two groups. Pick one region as your test group and keep the other as your control.

**Step 2:** Pause brand campaigns in the test region only. Keep everything else identical.

**Step 3:** Run the test for two to four weeks. You need enough data to be statistically meaningful, but not so long that seasonal shifts contaminate the results.

**Step 4:** Measure the difference. In the test region, look at organic click volume for brand terms. Compare the increase in organic clicks to the decrease in paid brand clicks. If organic picks up most of the slack, your brand campaigns weren't adding much.

### What to Measure

Track these metrics across both regions during the test period:

- **Organic clicks on brand terms** (via Google Search Console)
- **Paid clicks on brand terms** (via Google Ads)
- **Total clicks from brand searches** (organic plus paid combined)
- **Conversions from brand traffic** (both channels)
- **Revenue from brand traffic** (both channels)

If total clicks and conversions in the test region stay roughly flat while you stop paying for brand ads, you have your answer. The brand campaign was cannibalising organic, and you can safely reduce or eliminate the spend.

### Calculate Your True Incremental CPA

Once you have the test data, calculate the true cost per incremental acquisition from your brand campaigns. Take the total brand campaign spend and divide it by only the incremental conversions — the ones that wouldn't have happened without the ads.

If your brand campaign spent £2,000 and drove 100 conversions, but 80 of those would have come through organic anyway, your true incremental CPA isn't £20. It's £100. Suddenly that "cheap" brand campaign doesn't look so efficient.

## The Bottom Line

Brand bidding isn't inherently good or bad. It's a tactical decision that should be based on data, not dogma. The default position of "always bid on your brand" serves agencies more than it serves advertisers.

Test it. Measure the incrementality. Monitor your brand SERPs so you know when competitors are actually present. And redirect wasted brand spend to campaigns that bring in customers who don't already know your name.

That's how you grow.
